# Driving Coding Agents for Optimization Research — A Five-Session Cursus

*A narrative course design, in the instructor's voice. Biweekly, five sessions of roughly two hours each. This repository hosts everything.*

## The arc, and why it is shaped this way

I want to tell you what this cursus is before I tell you what is in it, because the shape is the point. Over five biweekly sessions we are going to walk one cohort of optimization and mathematical-science PhDs from "what is this thing actually doing when it predicts a token" all the way to a shared, competitive, deployed optimization tournament that none of you can win without everything you learned along the way. The connecting thread is a single discipline that I will repeat until it is reflex: **never raise an agent's autonomy past your ability to verify the *solution*, not just the code.** In our world the program can be green while the model is silently wrong — a flipped objective sense, a quietly dropped service-floor constraint, a binary relaxed to a continuous variable, a timeout misread as `Optimal`. Everything we build exists to catch that.

You arrive fresh. You are strong on the mathematics — you have formulated and solved your share of LPs and MILPs — but I assume no prior experience driving a coding agent, and nothing here depends on your having seen one before. Some of this material I have taught once, as a one-day [agentic-or-workshop](https://github.com/GuilhemBusset/agentic-or-workshop); this cursus rebuilds that foundation from scratch and then goes far past it, so you do not need to have attended it. That is exactly why Session 1 is a deliberate, honest re-baselining — the only session where we slow all the way down to first principles. It is also where the scaffolding we lean on all term gets set up from zero: the toolchain — `uv`, `mise`, `pytest`, the Claude Code and Codex CLIs — and the running example, a pack of supply-chain MILP problems (call them P01–P05) that we carry all the way to the final tournament.

The arc runs like this. We begin with **fundamentals**, so the whole room shares one small, correct picture of what a language model is and what an agent is. Then we study the **research agents** built from those same loops — teams of skills that read a thousand pages and draft you a comprehensive, cited document — because the first thing an OR scholar wants from this technology is a literature review and a write-up they can trust, and the first hard lesson is how *not* to trust them. From there we widen the lens to the full **spectrum of coding agents**, and we make the most consequential crossing in the field: from a local agent whose every diff you watch, to a remote agent that works in an ephemeral cloud sandbox and hands you back a pull request. Having earned the word "remote," we learn to **deploy** — to push to GitHub, let a chain of quality gates fire, and ship a reproducible study to AWS, with the solver-licensing wall as the reason our deploy story can never be a generic web-app deploy story. And finally we play **the game**: a cohort-wide optimization tournament where your private discipline becomes the public rule that decides who wins.

Each session is built to make the next one inevitable rather than magical. Watch for the hinges as we go — the context window in Session 1 that pre-loads everything about reproducibility and cost; the "drive then audit" muscle in Session 2 that you will need the instant a remote agent starts editing your repo unattended; the headless mode in Session 3 that turns out to be the entire secret of "remote"; the solve contract that travels from a private test in your repo to a public disqualifier in the final tournament. Let's begin.

## Session 1 — Fundamentals

Before we drive these things, we need a shared, honest picture of what they actually are, and I want to give it to you in your own language, because you will see the machinery faster than most. This is the only session where we go all the way down to first principles, and the discipline of the session is in what I *refuse* to teach. No backpropagation, no gradient descent, no transformer block internals, no RLHF, no benchmark numbers. The test for inclusion is brutal and simple: does this mental model change how you drive an agent next week? If not, it is cut.

The session splits cleanly into two questions, each hung on a single anchor framing chosen because you already own the underlying math. **What is an LLM?** I write it on the board the way you would write it yourself: a language model defines a learned conditional distribution $p(x_t \mid x_1, \dots, x_{t-1})$ over a fixed vocabulary, and generating text is just repeatedly sampling the next token, appending it, and recursing. That is autoregression — a factorization of a joint over sequences, identical in shape to objects you manipulate already. Everything hangs off that one line. The vocabulary is not letters or words but sub-word chunks, and the model never sees characters, only integer IDs — which is exactly why "how many r's in strawberry" fails: the model literally cannot see the letters inside a token. That is the first honest crack in the illusion of understanding, and the fix — *ask it to write code that counts* — quietly foreshadows the entire thesis of this course. Generation is sampling, not argmax; temperature is a one-parameter knob that reshapes the distribution, and it is why these systems are non-reproducible, a fact Sessions 3 and 4 will obsess over. As for attention, I give you the one framing that is both correct and instantly yours: it is a soft, differentiable database lookup, where the output is the *expectation* of value vectors under a query-dependent softmax measure. One sentence on permutation-equivariance, one sentence that multi-head means several such lookups in parallel, and we stop. The kernel and mean-field views are lovely optional reading; they are not live material.

The most important idea I will hand you all day is the second anchor: **the model is a pure function of its context, and the conversation is an illusion the harness maintains.** The model has no memory between calls. Each call, it is a pure function of the token sequence you hand it; the "conversation" you experience is the harness re-feeding the entire transcript every turn. From that single picture, four things fall out for free — the context window is a finite buffer that, on overflow, truncates and "forgets" and degrades; "in-context" means everything the model knows for this call is in the window right now, with no separate database it consults; cost and latency scale with transcript length, which is why agent sessions get expensive; and the KV cache is just memoization of an already-processed prefix, an argument any of you accepts in thirty seconds. This one framing pre-loads reproducibility, remote runs, and deployment cost without my having to name them yet. And it sets up the moral center of the whole cursus: because generation is always "sample a plausible next token," the model has no truth oracle and no reserved mass for "I don't know." It will emit a confident, beautifully formatted, *wrong* constraint with exactly the same fluency as a correct one. That is why every later session is about verifying the solution, not trusting the prose.

Then the agent half. What turns this sampler into something that edits your repo and runs your solver is almost nothing you would call intelligence — a *loop*. Perceive, plan, act, observe, repeat, until a stop condition fires. The shape is old (Russell and Norvig had it in 1995), and the LLM-era instantiation is ReAct — interleaving reasoning and acting. The deep point for this room: the loop keeps the model honest. A raw LLM hallucinates freely; an agent that must act on real tools and observe real results gets corrected by reality at every step. I use the clean definition — **Agent = Model + Harness**, and its corollary, "if you're not the model, you're the harness." The system prompt, the tool schemas, the filesystem access, the while-loop, and especially the stop conditions are all harness, with two failure modes worth naming: the runaway loop, and the model declaring victory while the tests are still red.

Two live demos do the heavy lifting, and they must be real, not slides. First, "it's just sampling, and it can't see letters": stream tokens so you *see* autoregression, toggle temperature so the same prompt diverges, run the strawberry letter-count failure, reveal the tokenization cause, then have it write code to count and watch it succeed — that pivot from guessing to running a tool is the entire course in miniature. Second, "the loop and the context bomb": point Claude Code at a tiny pre-seeded MILP with one deliberately flipped objective sense, watch a single loop turn read the file, propose a change, run pytest, and observe red, then deliberately overflow the context with a wall of noise and watch the agent forget an earlier instruction. We close by mapping both demos onto a compact reliability-controls taxonomy — boundary, context, contract, verification, traceability — using *this repo's own* `guard-main.sh` and `pr-only.yml` as live examples, so the abstraction is concrete and self-referential. The lab here is light by design — two demos and a mapping fit comfortably in two hours — and your home exercise is correspondingly spiral-setting: run the demos yourself, write three sentences on "sampling, not retrieval," and land your first pull request via `/ship`, so the protected-main muscle is exercised on something trivial before it matters.

## Session 2 — Research Agents: the team of skills behind a comprehensive document

Last session we built the loop. This session we point a *team* of those loops at the thing an OR scholar actually wants first — not code, but research: a literature review you can trust, a draft paper assembled from a pile of experiments, an agent that reaches a real database instead of guessing at it. I am deliberately *not* going to teach this as a tour of the commercial "deep research" buttons — Gemini Deep Research, OpenAI's, Perplexity. We will spend ten honest minutes on what that product category is — an agent that browses for minutes to hours and returns a cited report, whose quality scales with how much searching and reasoning you let it do, an anytime search over an information space with a stopping rule that any of you can read as exactly that — and, more importantly, on why those polished reports must never be trusted on faith. Then we spend the rest of the session somewhere far more useful for you: *inside* two open, inspectable systems you can read line by line and run on your own `uv` stack. Because both are built out of one primitive I introduce here — the **Skill**, a folder holding an instruction file and a few deterministic scripts that any of these agents can load on demand — and the moment you see that a "research assistant that runs in the background and hands you a comprehensive document" is really just a *team of skills and sub-agents*, the magic drains out of it and the engineering becomes yours.

The first is **PaperOrchestra** (`Ar9av/PaperOrchestra`), a faithful skill-pack implementation of a 2026 multi-agent paper-writing framework (Song et al., arXiv:2604.05018). You hand it an idea, an experimental log, a conference template and the page guidelines, and a team of agents turns them into a submission-ready LaTeX paper: an Outline agent plans the whole thing; a Plotting agent and a Literature-Review agent then run *in parallel* — a direct callback to Session 1's point that independent work needn't share a context window; a Section-Writing agent drafts the body in a single multimodal pass; and a Content-Refinement agent runs a simulated peer-review loop that accepts or reverts each edit under strict halt rules. What I want you to study is not that it writes prose — it is the *architecture*, because it is a master-class in the discipline of this entire course. The author draws one hard line through the system: anything that needs **judgment** (writing, searching, deciding) is delegated to the host agent's own model, while anything that can be made **deterministic** — Levenshtein-matching a cited title against Semantic Scholar, checking the date cutoff, deduplicating, enforcing the "≥90% of cited papers actually integrated" rule, grepping the final draft for an author name that would break double-blind anonymity, computing Citation-F1 — is a plain Python script that is *never wrong and never calls a model*. That is our verify-don't-trust thesis rendered as software: you do not *ask* the LLM to be careful with its citations, you put a citation gate in code that the model cannot talk its way past. And the pack ships its own **autoraters** — LLM-judges scoring citation precision/recall and literature-review quality on a rubric — which is the LLM-as-judge idea, now load-bearing inside a real pipeline rather than a throwaway slide.

There is a closing-the-loop trick in PaperOrchestra I love for this cohort: a pre-pipeline skill, the *agent-research-aggregator*, that walks your own Claude Code or Cursor history — the scattered logs of months of experiments — and synthesizes them into the clean `idea.md` and `experimental_log.md` the pipeline expects. The agent that helped you run your optimization experiments can hand its own transcript to the agent that writes them up. "It would have been fun if the repo wrote the paper," the README deadpans — and the serious point for you is that your agent logs *are* research provenance, and the distance between "I did the work" and "I wrote it up" has quietly become an automatable, auditable step.

The second system is **Google DeepMind's `science-skills`**, an official, Apache-2.0 bundle of agent skills that give a coding agent real hands in the scientific world: skills that query genomics, structural-biology and chemistry databases (UniProt, AlphaFold, ChEMBL, ClinVar), fold proteins, drive PyMOL, and — most relevant to you — search the literature through OpenAlex, EuropePMC and arXiv, all `uv`-managed with the same toolchain you set up in Session 1, plus a `workflow_skill_creator` skill that writes new skills. Where PaperOrchestra shows how research agents are *composed*, science-skills shows how they are *grounded*: each skill is a SKILL.md plus a script wrapping a real API, so the agent stops hallucinating a protein structure and instead *fetches* it. I am not turning you into bioinformaticians; I am showing you the template, because the OR analog writes itself — there is no `math.OC` equivalent of this bundle yet, and that absence is an opportunity your homework will exploit. (A small honesty note I'll make to the room: people will tell you one of these came "from a Chinese lab" — neither did. `science-skills` is Google DeepMind, and PaperOrchestra implements a Google-authored paper. Getting that provenance right is itself a Session-2 lesson: check the source of the claim before you repeat it.)

Now the failure-mode lesson lands with teeth, and I teach it *before* I let you be impressed, because both these systems are themselves honest about it — the whole reason they bristle with deterministic citation gates and autoraters is that the underlying model fabricates. Background research agents invent citations, over-retrieve until accuracy actually *falls*, and — the one that wounds a literature reviewer most — recall only a fraction of the canonical papers a domain expert would cite while confidently surfacing the periphery. The published audits put real, traceable numbers on this (citation and factual accuracy landing anywhere from roughly 40% to 80% *even when the links resolve*; expert-canon recall around a fifth), and I will hand you the exact papers so you re-derive those bands rather than take mine on faith — which is the entire point of a session about auditability. The discipline we name and keep is **claim-level auditability**: every load-bearing sentence traces to a verifiable source, and you check the synthesized claims, not just admire the ones that happen to carry a footnote. PaperOrchestra's citation gates and autoraters are simply this discipline mechanized; your job is to learn to demand it of any research agent, branded or open.

The lab is a single loop that turns both systems on *your own* work. Take a real optimization study — one of the P-pack problems — and run PaperOrchestra on it to produce a draft write-up; then turn its own deterministic gates and autoraters back on that draft, plus a small audit harness you write yourself, to measure citation validity, fabrication rate, and foundational-recall against an instructor-provided gold list, and write a one-page critique naming the single most dangerous wrong claim it made. Alongside that, in the science-skills style, you author **one** small "OR research skill" — a SKILL.md plus a `uv` script wrapping, say, an arXiv `math.OC` literature search or a standard MILP instance library — and feed its grounded output back into the pipeline, watching fabrication drop as the boundary tightens. That muscle — drive the background agent, then refuse to trust it until you have checked it — is exactly what you will need in Session 3, when these same skill-built agents stop running on your laptop and start running, unwatched, in the cloud.

## Session 3 — Types of Coding Agents: from Local Harnesses to Remote Fleets

By now you have lived inside one coding agent — Claude Code, on your own laptop, watching every diff scroll by, approving every tool call. This is the session where I pull the camera back and show you that "a coding agent" is not one thing but a *spectrum of harnesses*, and that the most consequential move in the whole field right now is the migration of that harness off the laptop and into ephemeral cloud sandboxes. I want you to leave able to draw the map from memory — local CLI, then headless/SDK, then remote cloud, then fleet/mesh — and to reason crisply about what *concretely* changes at each boundary. For an OR researcher this is not academic: it is the difference between an agent that can read your node-locked Xpress license and one that runs in a network-isolated VM where that license is dead on arrival. A note on time: the concept map is large, so the lab's first leg — running the same prompt headless on your own machine — is staged into pre-work, and the two hours live are spent on the remote crossing itself, the field contrast, and the five-axis worksheet.

I open by lining up the local harnesses you already half-know. Claude Code CLI is our anchor; alongside it sit OpenAI's Codex CLI (by now one face of a multi-interface Codex sharing state across CLI, IDE, and cloud), Google's Gemini CLI (Apache-2.0, a ReAct loop over built-in tools plus MCP), and aider (the git-commit-centric terminal pair-programmer). The defining trait of this rung is that the agent takes actions on *your* machine, with confirmation prompts, and you are in the loop synchronously. The pivot of the session is the realization that the *same local binary becomes the engine of everything remote* the moment you run it non-interactively. `claude -p "..."` runs the full agent loop once and exits with a status code your pipeline can branch on; `--output-format json` gives you one envelope, and `--output-format stream-json` gives newline-delimited events as they happen — which is precisely what makes a kill switch possible, because you can watch a bad event land and send a SIGINT. The Agent SDK wraps this with typed messages, tool-approval callbacks, and JSON-Schema structured outputs. The load-bearing teaching point: the GitHub Action and most cloud agents are headless mode with GitHub plumbing wired around it. Headless is the universal primitive; everything else is packaging — and our own repo's `/ship` and the audit workflow are exactly that plumbing, so "remote" should feel like a small step rather than a leap.

Then we cross. The remote harness model is one diagram I make you redraw from memory: an issue or prompt triggers a token-scoped repo clone into a fresh, network-isolated, disposable VM, where the agent reads, edits, tests, and iterates, then opens a draft PR, and the VM is destroyed. I walk the field so you can contrast shapes. The remote face of Claude Code is the official `anthropics/claude-code-action`: it runs the full runtime inside your own GitHub Actions runner, triggered by an `@claude` mention or an issue assignment, and Anthropic's 2026 hosted progression layers on top of it — Remote Control, then Remote Tasks, then cloud-hosted Routines (laptop off, agent still running), then Managed Agents (a platform that hands you the harness and a secure sandbox so you don't build the loop yourself). For contrast: OpenAI's Codex cloud runs network-isolated ephemeral sandboxes with `@codex` GitHub hooks, and the research-critical caveat for us is that it *disables internet and strips secrets during the agent phase*; GitHub's Copilot coding agent is GA, opens a draft PR, and crucially requires human approval before any CI/CD runs; Google's Jules offers a strong free tier on Google Cloud VMs; Cursor bundles editor and background agents from one vendor; and Devin is the broadest-scope "AI software engineer." The architecture lesson under all of them is the brain/hands split: the sandbox is stateless and secret-free by design ("the hands"), while credentials and conversation state live in a separate stateful control plane ("the brain"), and that separation is exactly what lets you spin up and kill a parallel fleet safely.

The last beat is what happens when you have *many* agents — ephemeral cloud sandboxes plus agent-mesh orchestration. Two complementary protocols anchor it, and these are the verifiable, load-bearing standards, so they are what I teach rather than any flashy runtime name: MCP is the *vertical* tool layer (agent to tools, data, memory), and A2A — Agent2Agent — is the *horizontal* layer where agents discover each other, delegate, and exchange results. A2A solves the N-by-M glue problem the way REST did for APIs; Google donated it to the Linux Foundation and by its one-year mark it had 150-plus organizations and production SDKs. The canonical pattern is "build with any framework, equip with MCP, communicate with A2A." When students want to *see* an orchestrator, I point them at real, inspectable open-source frameworks — LangGraph, CrewAI, AutoGen, and the reference A2A SDKs themselves — rather than the marketing-driven "swarm runtime" names that circulate online, several of which do not survive a search. The lesson is the pattern (vertical MCP + horizontal A2A), not a product to adopt. The real lesson at fleet scale is governance: non-human identity, where every agent needs a scoped, revocable credential; least-privilege that travels across delegation so an orchestrator does not silently hand its permissions downstream; and observability of who did what.

All of this culminates in the slide you keep: the five concrete things that change local to remote, each anchored to a solver consequence you feel in your bones. **Sandboxing** — your laptop's full filesystem and licenses become a deny-by-default microVM that blocks the cloud metadata endpoint and private ranges, and a node-locked Xpress or Gurobi license dies on arrival; only a floating internet service works, and Codex cloud kills even that. **Secrets and data egress** — secrets are injected at a proxy so the agent never sees them, tokens are short-lived and scoped, and the iron rule is that unpublished or embargoed datasets must not leave the machine (the most common real incident is not a sandbox escape but the agent pasting a key into its own scratch code and committing it). **Cost** — one agent session is many commits, which is many CI runs, which for us is many *solves*, and data-heavy swarms pay egress twice. **Review mode** — you stop watching a live diff and start reviewing an async PR with evidence, which is exactly why we insist the agent's self-verification be green before it surfaces a PR. And **determinism** — gone for the model, because temperature-zero is insufficient when floating-point addition is non-associative, so you shift to deterministic guardrails and full audit traces, and you assert on the objective within tolerance, not on the x-vector. The lab makes the boundary visceral: take one P0x task — default P01 — run it local in Claude Code, re-run the identical prompt headless to feel that it is the same engine, then file it as a GitHub issue, let a remote agent return a PR, and complete the five-axis worksheet. Was your Xpress license there? (No.) Did your private dataset reach the VM, and should it? What did the cost meter show? Did you review async with evidence? Was the result deterministic — and did you compare the objective rather than the x-vector? As a stretch, kick off two remote agents in parallel to feel the fleet, and inspect the scoped token each was granted. This lab deliberately pre-stages Session 4, where the issue's acceptance criteria literally *become* the relations the remote agent self-verifies before surfacing a PR.

## Session 4 — Deploying Code

Here is the framing I open with. For a working software engineer, "deploy" means a running service at a URL. For us it means something narrower and more honest: a merge to `main` that re-solves the canonical instance on a pinned *open* solver and regenerates the study artifact, behind a chain of quality gates that would not let a silently-wrong model through. Sessions 1 through 3 built the OR verification first — the solve contract, the metamorphic relations, the evals — and that is the whole reason I can now let the work leave the laptop safely. Deploying is not the dangerous step; deploying *without* the gate would be. On time: this is the fullest session, so we do the gate chain and the OIDC trust-policy lesson live (including reproducing the wildcard vulnerability), and the actual AWS deploy is a guided post-work exercise with a worked reference, so the two hours stay honest.

A deploy workflow has triggers, jobs that run in parallel by default and are sequenced with `needs:`, and permissions scoped per job. The mental model I want you to leave with is a sequenced gate chain where the deploy job declares every gate as a dependency, so deploy is structurally *unreachable* until each is green and each is registered as a required status check on protected `main`. The order is shift-left, cheapest and highest-signal first: secret scanning over the *full git history* with `fetch-depth: 0` (a different discipline from SAST, and frequently conflated with it); lint and type-check, including `actionlint` on the workflow YAML itself because it catches expression type errors and script-injection that a generic YAML linter never sees; static analysis with CodeQL; dependency and supply-chain checks; then *the gate generic CI never has* — the solve contract and metamorphic suite as a required check, where status must be in a whitelist rather than a bare equality to `Optimal`, an independent constraint re-check runs in our own code, integrality round-trips, the gap is bounded, and the objective reproduces within tolerance; then a multi-arch container build scanned with Trivy; then IaC validation with Checkov (worth a 2026 footnote: tfsec was folded into Trivy and Terrascan was archived, so Checkov is the policy source of truth); and finally a deploy gated behind a GitHub Environment with required reviewers and a branch restriction. Crucially we separate "wrong" — a status, feasibility, or invariant failure, which is hard red — from "slow" — a gap not closed under the cap, which is a warning or quarantine — so a correct-but-hard model is not rejected. Supply-chain hardening is woven through, not bolted on, because of the March 2025 incident where mutable tags were rewritten on an action used by tens of thousands of repos and SHA-pinned workflows were the ones left untouched; so we pin every third-party action to a full commit SHA, default the token to read-only, keyless-sign the image with Cosign, emit SLSA provenance, and run OpenSSF Scorecard weekly.

Then we deploy to AWS, and the part I make everyone do by hand once is OIDC keyless authentication. You create an IAM OIDC identity provider for GitHub's token endpoint, then a role whose trust policy pins the `sub` claim to your exact repo and branch — and a wildcard there is a *real* vulnerability, which I make you reproduce so the lesson sticks; in the workflow you grant `id-token: write` and use the configure-credentials action, and the classic "could not load credentials from any providers" failure is almost always a missing `id-token: write`. The compute choice maps to the OR workload, not to fashion: a static results dashboard on a private S3 bucket served through CloudFront is the cheapest and most reproducible default; Lambda container images for fast sub-fifteen-minute solves; ECS Fargate or App Runner for a steady synchronous `/solve` API with longer solves; and AWS Batch on EC2 Spot for the heaviest async MILP solves. The deploy mechanics on the ECS path are build, push to ECR tagged with the commit SHA, render the task definition, roll the service, and roll back if the smoke test drifts.

This is also the session where the solver-licensing reality becomes a first-class teaching point, because it is the single reason an OR curriculum cannot inherit a web-app deploy story. Proprietary solvers are local-only on ephemeral runners by default: a node-locked or static license binds to the first container and dies on the next fresh run. The only supported cloud path is an internet floating service — Gurobi's WLS with token-based credentials stored as secrets and outbound HTTPS to its token endpoint, or FICO Xpress's newer key-based licensing — and most academic licenses lack that entitlement, while Codex cloud strips even that by disabling internet during the agent phase. So the iron rule for CI, cloud, and deploy is *open solvers, always*: HiGHS by default, OR-Tools CP-SAT for combinatorial work, CBC and PuLP as fallback, accepting that they run ten to a hundred times slower on hard MILP, which is why the cloud runs small instances and the big proprietary solves stay on your laptop or a self-hosted institutional runner. The deeper lesson lands here: the solver *version* is part of your reproducible environment, and your published study must reproduce on an open solver even if you developed against a commercial one.

The agent's role flips in this session. It stops writing application code and starts writing the deployment substrate — the workflow YAML, the multi-stage non-root pinned Dockerfile, the Terraform or CDK, and least-privilege IAM with a human-readable justification per permission — and it reviews `terraform plan` or `cdk synth` in headless mode with its tools restricted to `Bash` and `Read` to limit blast radius. It runs *in* CI via the first-party Claude Code GitHub Action. And the discipline from the earlier sessions carries through exactly: the agent's own YAML and IaC are gated by the same chain it cannot bypass — it cannot ship a static-key workflow, cannot bypass branch protection, and cannot merge a model that fails the solve contract. The lab is the rite of passage. The agent generates the deploy artifacts; you set up OIDC by hand and watch it fail first with a missing `id-token: write` then pass; you `/ship` a PR and watch the gate chain, with the solve-contract gate going *red* on a planted dropped-constraint fixture whose objective looks deceptively *better* (caught by the independent feasibility re-check); then, in guided post-work, you approve the Environment, build and push or sync to S3, watch the reproducibility smoke test cold-start and re-solve a canned instance, hit a live URL, and finally trigger a one-command rollback when a deliberately-bumped unpinned solver version drifts the objective beyond tolerance. We close on cost hygiene — `terraform destroy`, instance-size and timeout caps on the public endpoint, because the people who get the surprise bill are researchers on personal AWS accounts.

## Session 5 — The Arena: the capstone as a cohort-wide optimization tournament

Across four sessions we have walked one ladder, and every rung of it has been a *private* virtue. You learned to trust a PR you review later rather than a diff you watch live. You learned to drive a research agent and then audit its citations. You learned to cross from local to remote and reason about what changes. You learned to deploy behind a gate chain that makes a silently-wrong model structurally unreachable. The capstone makes all of it *public*. We take everything — your model, your harness, your remote-agent loop, your reproducible artifact — and put it in a single shared repo where it must survive contact with twenty other people's models on instances none of you have seen. The course's whole discipline becomes the only way to win: a policy that is fast but silently infeasible does not place low, it is *disqualified* by the very solve contract you wrote back in Session 1.

The Arena is an agent-driven optimization tournament, and I chose it because it has a near-exact OR-native precedent — the EURO Meets NeurIPS 2022 VRP competition, with containerized submissions, a static-plus-dynamic dual rank, and a weekly leaderboard. There is one shared Arena repo. The instructor owns and protects the `engine/`: a discrete-event simulator that replays a problem instance as a stream of events (for P01, linehaul demand and charger availability revealed wave-by-wave over a rolling horizon), a scorer built from the `or_verify/` solve contract and metamorphic suite plus a cost meter, and the leaderboard CI. Each student owns exactly one directory behind a frozen `Policy` interface whose `act` method decides which trucks, routes, and charges to commit each wave. This is a *dynamic* problem on purpose: a static one-shot MILP just rewards whoever has the biggest solver, which you already know how to win, whereas a rolling-horizon stream rewards a genuine policy — re-optimizing each wave under uncertainty about future arrivals. The policy may call any solver behind the solver-agnostic seam, but in the Arena it is open solvers only, because the Arena runs in CI and on the public deploy path, with Xpress staying on your laptop — the exact licensing reality of Session 4. Each policy gets a per-wave wall-clock deadline and a total compute budget: the Session 3 and 4 kill-switch, now a rule of the game, which deliberately makes raw solver muscle non-dominant.

Here is how every prior session becomes load-bearing rather than ornamental. You cannot even *enter* without Session 1 and 4, because your policy must pass the solve contract in CI to be scored at all. You cannot *stay on the board* without reproducibility, because nondeterminism beyond tolerance is a disqualifier. You cannot *improve safely* without your invariants, because they are what keep a remote agent's "improvement" from being a silent regression. And you cannot *iterate at speed* without the remote-agent loop — which is exactly how you climb between sessions: you file an issue against your own policy ("reduce p90 cost on the high-variance public instances without violating demand-monotonicity"), assign it to a headless remote agent in an ephemeral runner, and the agent self-verifies against the solve contract and metamorphic suite *before* opening the PR, because Session 3 encoded what "correct" means as runnable gates. You review the claim-with-evidence, request a change, merge, and the leaderboard updates on merge. The competition literally runs on the Session 4 deploy pipeline. And you can reach back to Session 2 to strategize: run a background research agent (a PaperOrchestra-style literature skill) to survey rolling-horizon dispatch and look-ahead policies, then feed that synthesis into the issue you file. Research agent, then coding agent, then leaderboard — the whole toolchain in one loop.

The single sharpest teaching beat — and the reason the Arena is the right capstone — is the public-versus-private split, the Kaggle idea ported to OR. You tune against a public instance set with a live leaderboard for the tuning weeks, but final standings run on a *private* held-out stream revealed only on Demo Day. The "shake-up" *is* the lesson: the student who treated the public board as ground truth and overfit their warm-starts drops, and the one whose metamorphic invariants enforced a genuinely robust model climbs. Watching the rankings scramble live teaches the generalization lesson — a green board is necessary, not sufficient — far better than any lecture, and it is the OR translation of the benchmark-contamination story the whole field is living through right now — the same distrust-the-leaderboard reflex we built in Session 2. Scoring is multi-objective and OR-native: the primary rank is mean and p90 solution cost on the private stream under the hard caps, with hard disqualifiers for any solve-contract failure (infeasible-reported-feasible, status misread, integrality silently relaxed) or any metamorphic violation, so "fast but wrong" loses to "correct but slower" by construction — the wrong-versus-slow split of Session 1 is now the tournament rulebook. Branch protection, `/ship`, and `guard-main.sh` — already live in *this* repo — are the integrity layer: no direct pushes to the engine, every policy lands via reviewed PR, no `--no-verify` and no `--force`. On Demo Day each of you gives a three-minute talk: walk your policy, show your `RESULTS.md` reproducing the recorded objective, scroll a PR history that visibly spans all five rungs (at least one local-authored, at least one red-then-green by the leaderboard CI, at least one authored by a remote agent from an issue), and explain the one design decision that moved you on the *private* board.

## What you carry away

If I have done my job, you leave with one reflex and a working spine. The reflex is the discipline that has run through every session: never raise an agent's autonomy past your ability to verify the solution, not just the code — because the model is a fluent sampler with no truth oracle, and a beautifully formatted wrong constraint reads exactly like a correct one. The spine is concrete and yours: a model behind a solver-agnostic seam, a solve contract and metamorphic suite that catch silent wrongness oracle-free, a remote-agent loop that lets you improve a model from a filed issue, a deploy pipeline that regenerates a citable reproducible study on every merge, and the judgment to audit a deep-research report instead of admiring it. You arrived able to formulate a MILP on your laptop. You leave able to drive a fleet of agents across the full research-software lifecycle of a real optimization study — and, in the Arena, you will have proven it the only way that counts, by shipping a policy that generalizes, reproducibly, against the whole cohort, on instances you never saw.

## Appendix: the live spine in this repository

This is not a paper course; the guardrail spine is real and running here today. `scripts/guard-main.sh` is a shared PreToolUse guard — wired by both Claude Code (`.claude/settings.json`) and Codex (`.codex/config.toml`) — that refuses any Bash command that would commit on or push to `main`/`master` and blocks `--no-verify` and `--force`. `.githooks/pre-commit` and `pre-push` enforce the same at the git layer. `.github/workflows/pr-only.yml` audits that every commit landing on `main` arrived via a merged PR. The `/ship` skill (`.claude/skills/ship/SKILL.md` for Claude Code, `.agents/skills/ship/SKILL.md` for Codex, both driving the shared `scripts/ship.sh`) rebases, pushes a feature branch, and opens or updates the PR. `.claude/settings.json` holds the deny-list as a belt-and-braces net. Students inherit all of this on day one and do not build it; it is the boundary and traceability layer that makes every "land it via a reviewed PR" instruction in this cursus literally enforced rather than merely requested — and in Session 5 it becomes the integrity layer of the game itself.
